Mess hall navigation
How to eat well from a buffet-style setup

The plate rule: Build your plate in order — protein first, vegetables second, carbohydrates last. One trip through the buffet. What you load last, you eat least of. Water before and during every meal.

⬤ Protein · ½ plate
⬤ Vegetables · ¼ plate
⬤ Carbohydrates · ¼ plate
Protein anchors

Go-to picks at any mess

Aim for two palm-sized serves of protein per meal. Choose cuts that are sliced or grilled — avoid anything in a sauce or stew.

  • Chicken breast
  • Beef — eye fillet, sirloin, topside or silverside
  • Lamb — leg of lamb
  • Pork — loin or tenderloin
  • Eggs — boiled
  • Grilled fish
  • Legumes — beans, lentils (plant-based option)
Smart carbohydrates

Fuel without overdoing it

  • Rice — one cupped handful
  • Pasta — one cupped handful
  • Potato — one fist-sized portion
  • Sweet potato — one fist-sized portion
  • Fresh fruit — unrestricted
  • Rolled oats — ½ cup dry
Navigating the buffet

Tips for the buffet line

  • Fill half your plate with vegetables and/or salad before anything else
  • Avoid sauces, dressings and gravies — hidden calories add up fast
Mindset shift: Every meal on site is a choice. The workers who eat with intention are the ones who perform and recover well — every swing, every time.
02 — Meal prep
📦
Meal prep strategies
For self-catering on site. All recommendations are a guide — apply specifics as discussed with your coach.
Foundation method

Cook once, eat 4–5 days

  • Batch cook one protein (e.g. 1.5kg chicken breast)
  • Batch cook rice, pasta, potato or sweet potato
  • Fresh vegetables of choice — unrestricted
  • Season and rotate sauces to taste — apply dressings fresh daily to prevent burnout on roster
Prep day template

2-hour session before you fly

  • Proteins: chicken, mince
  • Carbohydrates: rice, pasta, potato or sweet potato
  • Fresh vegetables of choice — unrestricted
  • Snacks: YoPRO Perform pouch, protein bar, rice cakes and tinned tuna
  • Label containers with day and meal
Portioning your meals

How to portion on roster

  • Pre-portion meals before storing
  • Use smaller containers and cutlery where possible — it naturally limits portion size
  • Prep salad bases — bag and store separately
  • Season and rotate sauces to taste — apply dressings fresh daily to prevent burnout on roster
Pre-shift preparation

Setting yourself up before the shift

  • Always have a pre-shift meal prepared and ready to go
  • Don't rely on willpower on roster — have food ready before fatigue sets in
  • The workers who prepare are the ones who perform — food ready is half the battle won
Meal prep before you fly — every day without food ready is a day left to chance. The swing is won or lost before you even land on site.
03 — On-shift
🎒
On-shift nutrition
Snacks, hydration and meal timing on site
On-shift nutrition

Portable, practical, high protein

  • Protein bar — 20g+ protein, <10g fat, low sugar, ~200 calories
  • Rice cakes + tinned tuna
  • Fresh fruit of choice — unrestricted
  • Hard-boiled eggs (prepped in advance)
  • Beef jerky or biltong (30g serve) — 15g+ protein, <3g fat, <400mg sodium
  • Protein shake — 25g+ protein, <5g fat, <5g sugar, ~130–150 calories (mix with water if using powder)
Hydration

Heat + physical work = high demand

  • Minimum 3–4L water per shift in the heat
  • Electrolyte tabs, sachets or sugar free Gatorade/Powerade
  • Avoid relying on coffee as hydration
  • Flavour water with lemon or sugar free cordial if needed
  • Target clear urine colour
Meal timing

Timing your meals on shift

  • Aim to eat every 3–4 hours throughout the day
  • Never go longer than 5 hours without a meal or snack
  • Consistent meal timing helps regulate hunger and energy on roster
  • Don't skip breakfast — under-eating on roster kills recovery and sets the day back before it starts
  • Adjust your eating window around training as discussed
Pack your on-shift nutrition before you fly — protein bar, jerky, rice cakes and tinned tuna, electrolyte tabs. Keep it in your work bag and restock each swing. When you're tired and hungry on shift, zero friction between you and the right choice is everything.
04 — Sleep & recovery
😴
Sleep & recovery
The overlooked pillar of performance and body composition
Why it matters

Sleep directly impacts your results

  • Poor sleep raises ghrelin — the hormone that drives hunger
  • Less than 7 hours increases cravings for high-calorie foods
  • Sleep is when muscle repair and growth occurs
  • Chronic poor sleep elevates cortisol, which promotes fat storage
  • Energy and focus on shift decline massively without at least 6 hours of sleep each night
On roster habits

Making sleep work on site

  • Aim for 7–9 hours every night, even on roster
  • Keep a consistent bedtime — your body clock responds to routine
  • Use an eye mask in camp rooms to block out light
  • Earplugs or white noise to block camp noise
  • Avoid screens at least 30 minutes before bed
Nutrition & sleep

What you eat affects how you sleep

  • Avoid large meals within 2 hours of bedtime (where possible)
  • Limit caffeine within 6 hours of your intended sleep time — regardless of shift
  • Alcohol disrupts sleep quality even if it helps you fall asleep
  • A small protein snack before bed can support overnight recovery
Shift transitions

Flying in and out

  • Adjust your sleep schedule 1–2 days before a roster change
  • Get sunlight exposure on arrival to reset your body clock
  • Avoid napping during the day
  • Prioritise sleep on your first night on site above everything else
The bottom line: You can eat perfectly and train hard, but chronic poor sleep will undermine both. On roster, protecting your sleep is as important as protecting your nutrition.
